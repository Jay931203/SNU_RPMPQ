\begin{abstract}
In frequency-division duplex (FDD) massive multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) systems, downlink CSI must be estimated at the user equipment (UE) and fed back to the base station (BS). In practice, UE-side inference is constrained by latency, energy, and compute, making lightweight encoders and low-precision execution unavoidable. Beyond reconstruction metrics such as normalized mean-squared error (NMSE), CSI distortion under UE constraints directly translates into transmission-rate degradation and outage events when the reconstructed CSI is used for precoding and link adaptation. To handle long-tailed delay--angular CSI under strict UE budgets, we propose an asymmetric CSI feedback autoencoder that employs a lightweight state-space model (SSM) encoder at the UE, exploiting soft locality and natural quantization robustness, paired with a high-capacity Transformer decoder at the BS. Building on this architecture, we develop Reliability-Preserving Mixed-Precision Quantization Scheduling (RP-MPQ): a state-conditioned importance surface quantifies the expected downstream reliability shortfall from each block-bit assignment given the wireless operating state; offline, per-state optimal mixed-precision policies are constructed from this surface under a long-term UE compute budget, and online deployment reduces to a single table lookup with no runtime optimization. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed asymmetric architecture improves the accuracy--complexity trade-off and that RP-MPQ substantially reduces rate-based outage events under the same average UE-side budget.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =========================================================

\subsection{Background and Motivation}
In frequency-division duplex (FDD) massive multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) systems, CSI feedback is essential for downlink precoding and rate adaptation. Unlike time-division duplex systems, FDD operation does not permit channel reciprocity; therefore, the UE must estimate downlink CSI and convey a compressed representation to the BS over a limited feedback link. Recent deep-learning-based CSI feedback methods employ autoencoders to learn compact latent representations that can be quantized and transmitted efficiently \cite{CsiNet,CsiNetPlus,CRNet,CLNet,TransNet,ACCsiNet}.

A practical bottleneck is UE-side feasibility at inference time. CSI compression must operate under strict latency and energy constraints, which often forces lightweight encoder architectures and low-precision execution. Under such operation, CSI distortion is unavoidable and its impact is not limited to reconstruction error: distorted CSI directly affects downlink transmission rates and increases outage events when used for precoding and link adaptation \cite{Jindal2006}. This motivates CSI feedback designs that explicitly account for transmission-rate-based reliability, rather than optimizing reconstruction metrics alone.

\subsection{Asymmetric CSI Feedback Architecture}
In the delay--angular domain, CSI often exhibits localized energy around dominant propagation paths while retaining non-negligible long-range components due to off-grid effects and finite DFT resolution. Encoders that impose hard locality (e.g., limited receptive fields) may effectively truncate such components under strict budgets, incurring irreversible structural loss. In contrast, state-space model (SSM) encoders aggregate inputs through soft memory with exponentially decaying influence, allowing long-range dependencies to be attenuated rather than abruptly truncated. Moreover, SSM encoders with contractive state dynamics provide natural robustness to low-precision execution. Recent work also highlights emerging SSM opportunities in wireless communications \cite{Mamba,MambaWireless}.

Motivated by this structural match and the inherent UE--BS computational asymmetry, we propose an asymmetric CSI feedback architecture that places a lightweight SSM-based encoder at the UE and a higher-capacity Transformer decoder at the BS.

\subsection{Reliability-Preserving Mixed-Precision Quantization}
On top of the fixed asymmetric architecture, we develop Reliability-Preserving Mixed-Precision Quantization Scheduling (RP-MPQ), which enables runtime, reliability-aware precision adaptation with minimal online complexity.

RP-MPQ defines a state-conditioned importance surface that measures the expected downstream reliability shortfall from each block-bit assignment, conditioned on a wireless state summarizing SNR and CSI structure. Offline, this surface determines per-state optimal mixed-precision policies under a compute budget; online, the UE performs a single table lookup to deploy the pre-computed policy with no runtime optimization.

\subsection{Contributions}
The main contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
\item An asymmetric CSI feedback autoencoder using a lightweight SSM-based UE encoder and a high-capacity BS decoder, exploiting soft locality and contractive state dynamics for quantization-robust compression of long-tailed delay--angular CSI.
\item RP-MPQ, a state-conditioned importance framework that constructs per-state optimal mixed-precision policies offline from a downstream reliability shortfall surface, with online deployment via pure table lookup under a long-term UE budget.
\end{itemize}

\section{System Model and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:system_model}
% =========================================================

\subsection{System Model}
\label{subsec:system_model}
We consider an FDD massive MIMO downlink system where a BS with $N_t$ transmit antennas serves a UE with $N_r$ receive antennas. Let $\mathbf{H}[n] \in \mathbb{C}^{N_r \times N_t}$ denote the downlink MIMO channel on the $n$-th OFDM subcarrier, $n=0,\ldots,N_f-1$.

Applying an $N_f$-point unitary inverse DFT (IDFT) across the $N_f$ subcarriers yields the delay-domain channel taps
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{H}_d[\ell]
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{N_f}} \sum_{n=0}^{N_f-1} \mathbf{H}[n] e^{j 2\pi n\ell/N_f},
\quad \ell=0,\ldots,N_f-1.
\label{eq:delay_idft}
\end{equation}
Each tap is mapped to the angular domain using unitary DFT matrices
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}[\ell] = \mathbf{F}_r \mathbf{H}_d[\ell] \mathbf{F}_t^{\mathsf{H}},
\label{eq:ang_transform}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{F}_r \in \mathbb{C}^{N_r \times N_r}$ and $\mathbf{F}_t \in \mathbb{C}^{N_t \times N_t}$ are unitary DFT matrices.

Due to limited delay spread, most energy is concentrated in the first $N_a \le N_f$ delay taps. We retain
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_a \triangleq \big[ \mathbf{X}[0], \mathbf{X}[1], \ldots, \mathbf{X}[N_a-1] \big].
\label{eq:truncate}
\end{equation}

At the UE, an encoder $f_\theta(\cdot)$ maps $\mathbf{X}_a$ to a latent vector
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z} = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}_a), \quad \mathbf{z}\in\mathbb{R}^{D}, \quad D \ll 2 N_a N_r N_t.
\label{eq:encoder}
\end{equation}
(Complex CSI is represented in a real-valued format, e.g., by stacking real and imaginary parts; we omit this bookkeeping for clarity.)

The compression ratio is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CR} \triangleq \frac{D}{2 N_a N_r N_t}.
\label{eq:cr}
\end{equation}

The latent is quantized and transmitted over the feedback link,
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}} = Q_{\mathrm{fb}}(\mathbf{z}),
\label{eq:latent_quant}
\end{equation}
and the BS reconstructs the truncated CSI as
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{X}}_a = g_\phi(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}).
\label{eq:decoder}
\end{equation}

\subsection{UE Constraints and Design Perspective}
\label{subsec:constraints}
In practical deployments, CSI feedback is constrained by UE-side latency, energy, and compute limits, while the BS typically has substantially greater computational resources. This UE--BS asymmetry motivates architectures with a lightweight UE-side encoder and a higher-capacity BS-side decoder.

We study the joint design of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a UE-feasible asymmetric encoder--decoder architecture that improves the accuracy--complexity trade-off; and
\item a runtime mixed-precision scheduling mechanism for the UE-side encoder that suppresses transmission-rate-based reliability violations under a long-term average UE compute budget.
\end{enumerate}
Section~\ref{sec:asym_arch} describes the architecture, and Sections~\ref{sec:offline}--\ref{sec:online} develop the mixed-precision scheduling framework.

\section{Asymmetric CSI Feedback Architecture}
\label{sec:asym_arch}
% =========================================================

\subsection{Long-Tailed Locality of Delay--Angular Domain CSI}
\label{subsec:longtail}
In the delay--angular domain, CSI energy is often concentrated around dominant angular components associated with physical propagation paths. However, locality is not strictly compact: when a physical angle $\theta_0$ falls between DFT bins, the angular-domain coefficient at bin $k$ equals $|X[k]|=|a_0|\,|D_{N_t}(\theta_0-k/N_t)|$, where
\begin{equation}
D_N(f)=\frac{\sin(\pi N f)}{N\sin(\pi f)}
\label{eq:dirichlet_kernel}
\end{equation}
is the Dirichlet kernel. For offset $d\triangleq|k-k_0|\ge 1$, the standard bound $|\sin(\pi f)|\ge 2|f|$ for $|f|\le 1/2$ yields $|D_N|\le 1/(\pi N|f|)$, giving the oscillatory long tail:
\begin{equation}
x_{i_0+d} \;\approx\; c_0\,\frac{\eta_d}{1+d},
\qquad
d \triangleq |i-i_0|\ge 0,
\label{eq:dirichlet_osc_tail}
\end{equation}
where $\eta_d$ is an oscillatory factor satisfying $|\eta_d|\le 1$, and $c_0>0$ absorbs the path gain and normalization constants. In particular, the magnitude envelope obeys
\begin{equation}
|x_i|
\;\lesssim\;
\frac{c_0}{1+|i-i_0|}.
\label{eq:longtail_bound}
\end{equation}
Thus, distant angular coefficients can remain non-negligible, and strict hard locality may cause irreversible structural loss under tight UE budgets.

\subsection{Hard-Locality Encoding and Structural Truncation Loss}
\label{subsec:hard_locality}
Encoders based on CNNs impose finite receptive fields and thus a hard-locality constraint. Let $L$ denote an effective receptive-field radius. Under the envelope in \eqref{eq:longtail_bound}, the residual tail energy beyond radius $L$ satisfies
\begin{align}
E_{\mathrm{hard}}(L)
\triangleq
\sum_{|i-i_0|>L} |x_i|^2
\;\lesssim\;
c_0^2\sum_{d=L+1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(1+d)^2}
\;\le\;
\frac{c_0^2}{1+L}
=
\mathcal{O}\!\left(L^{-1}\right),
\quad d \triangleq |i-i_0|.
\label{eq:hard_tail}
\end{align}
The bound indicates that reducing truncation loss requires substantially increasing $L$ (e.g., deeper/wider CNNs), which increases UE-side compute and latency.

\subsection{Soft-Locality Encoding via State-Space Aggregation}
\label{subsec:ssm_soft}
State-space model (SSM) encoders provide soft locality: rather than hard-truncating long-range coefficients, they recursively aggregate inputs with exponentially decaying aggregation weights. A linear time-invariant SSM can be written as
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{s}_{k+1}=\mathbf{A}\mathbf{s}_k+\mathbf{B}\mathbf{u}_k,
\quad
\mathbf{y}_k=\mathbf{C}\mathbf{s}_k,
\label{eq:ssm}
\end{equation}
which yields the convolutional form
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}_k=\sum_{d\ge 0}\mathbf{C}\mathbf{A}^{d}\mathbf{B}\mathbf{u}_{k-d}.
\label{eq:ssm_conv}
\end{equation}
If $\mathbf{A}$ is stable, then there exist constants $c_1>0$ and $\alpha>0$ such that $\|\mathbf{A}^{d}\|\le c_1 e^{-\alpha d}$ for all $d\ge 0$.
To compare hard truncation and soft aggregation on the same footing, define the aggregated tail contribution beyond horizon $L$:
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{soft}}(L)
\triangleq
\left\|
\mathbf{C}\sum_{d>L}\mathbf{A}^{d}\mathbf{B}\mathbf{u}_{k-d}
\right\|.
\label{eq:soft_contrib_def}
\end{equation}
Motivated by Dirichlet-type leakage, we model the input envelope as $\|\mathbf{u}_{k-d}\|\lesssim \frac{c_0}{1+d}$ (up to oscillations as in \eqref{eq:dirichlet_osc_tail}). Then
\begin{align}
R_{\mathrm{soft}}(L)
&\le
\|\mathbf{C}\|\|\mathbf{B}\|\sum_{d>L}\|\mathbf{A}^{d}\|\,\|\mathbf{u}_{k-d}\|
\;\lesssim\;
c_3\sum_{d=L+1}^{\infty}\frac{e^{-\alpha d}}{1+d}
=
\mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{e^{-\alpha L}}{1+L}\right),
\label{eq:soft_tail_bound}
\end{align}
for some constant $c_3>0$. This quantifies that the incremental influence of very distant coefficients is suppressed under exponential aggregation weights, while those coefficients are still retained and recursively aggregated rather than being abruptly truncated.

\subsection{Quantization Robustness}
\label{subsec:ssm_quant_robust}

UE-side inference requires low-precision execution, and post-training quantization introduces parameter perturbations $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}=\boldsymbol{\theta}+\Delta\boldsymbol{\theta}$.
In Mamba's parameterization, the discretized state matrix is diagonal with entries $\bar{A}_{t,ii}=\exp(-\Delta_{t,i}\,\mathrm{softplus}(A_{\mathrm{param},i}))$. Since both $\Delta_{t,i}>0$ and $\mathrm{softplus}(\cdot)>0$, every entry satisfies $0<\bar{A}_{t,ii}<1$, making the state recursion inherently contractive for all inputs.
Unlike feedforward stacking where quantization errors accumulate with depth, a contractive state recursion prevents perturbation amplification: the output deviation converges to a finite steady-state bound independent of sequence length.
This robustness property is structurally consistent with the soft-locality analysis in Section~\ref{subsec:ssm_soft}: the same exponential decay enabling efficient long-tail modeling also stabilizes quantization-induced distortions under UE-side low-precision execution.

\subsection{Asymmetric Architecture}
\label{subsec:asym_conclude}

Based on these observations, we adopt an asymmetric CSI feedback architecture that deploys a lightweight Mamba-style SSM encoder at the UE \cite{Mamba, VMamba} and a higher-capacity Transformer-based decoder at the BS \cite{TransNet}.
The UE encoder benefits from SSM's linear-time complexity and quantization robustness, while the BS decoder leverages Transformer's expressive capacity without UE-side constraints.
This asymmetry is a practical design choice matched to the CSI feedback channel's inherent compute imbalance between UE and BS.

\section{RP-MPQ: Offline Policy Construction}
\label{sec:offline}
% =========================================================

This section develops the offline stage of RP-MPQ. A \emph{policy} $\pi=(b_1,\ldots,b_M)$ assigns bit-widths $b_m\in\mathcal{B}$ to the $M$ quantizable encoder blocks. Mixed precision is applied to encoder weights while activations remain fixed at INT16. The feedback-link quantization $Q_{\mathrm{fb}}(\cdot)$ in \eqref{eq:latent_quant} is kept fixed. RP-MPQ constructs a \emph{state-conditioned importance surface} measuring the expected downstream reliability loss from each block-bit assignment, and derives per-state optimal policies from this surface.

\subsection{Mixed-Precision Policy Space}
\label{subsec:policy_space}

Let the UE-side encoder consist of $M$ quantizable blocks. A mixed-precision policy is a block-wise assignment of bit-widths
\begin{equation}
\pi \triangleq (b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_M),
\label{eq:policy_def}
\end{equation}
where $b_m \in \mathcal{B}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ denotes the discrete set of supported bit-widths. The induced policy space has cardinality
\begin{equation}
|\Pi| = |\mathcal{B}|^M,
\label{eq:policy_cardinality}
\end{equation}
which makes exhaustive evaluation infeasible for moderate $M$.

For a policy $\pi$, the normalized UE-side cost is
\begin{equation}
\kappa_{\pi} \triangleq \sum_{m=1}^{M} \kappa_m(b_m) \in (0,1],
\label{eq:kappa_policy_sum}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_m(b_m)$ denotes the compute cost of executing block $m$ at bit-width $b_m$ (e.g., BOPs), normalized such that $\kappa_{\pi^{(\mathrm{FP32})}}=1$.

\subsection{Wireless State}
\label{subsec:wireless_state}

We define a two-dimensional wireless state $\boldsymbol{\xi}=(\rho,\zeta)$ summarizing the current operating condition, where $\rho$ is the received SNR and $\zeta$ captures CSI structure.

For the structural component, define the normalized energy map
\begin{equation}
P_{ij}(\mathbf{X}_a) = \frac{|X_{ij}|^2}{\sum_{u,v}|X_{uv}|^2},
\label{eq:energy_map}
\end{equation}
and soft-locality kernels aligned with the encoder's receptive structure:
\begin{equation}
[K_d]_{i,i'} = \frac{(1+|i-i'|)^{-\alpha_d}}{\sum_{i''}(1+|i-i''|)^{-\alpha_d}}, \qquad
[K_a]_{j,j'} = \frac{(1+|j-j'|)^{-\alpha_a}}{\sum_{j''}(1+|j-j''|)^{-\alpha_a}},
\label{eq:kernels}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_d,\alpha_a>0$ are decay exponents along the delay and angular axes.
The \emph{full} structural mismatch index is
\begin{equation}
\zeta_{\mathrm{full}}(\mathbf{X}_a) = 1 - \operatorname{tr}\!\left(P^\top K_d\, P\, K_a\right),
\label{eq:zeta_full}
\end{equation}
which requires two matrix products costing $\mathcal{O}(N_a^2 N_t + N_a N_t^2)$.

For efficient UE-side computation, we introduce a \emph{proxy} mismatch index based on per-axis marginals.
Define the delay and angular marginal energy distributions
\begin{equation}
[\mathbf{p}_d]_i = \sum_{j} P_{ij}, \qquad
[\mathbf{p}_a]_j = \sum_{i} P_{ij},
\label{eq:marginals}
\end{equation}
and per-axis compactness scores
\begin{equation}
c_d = \mathbf{p}_d^\top K_d\, \mathbf{p}_d, \qquad
c_a = \mathbf{p}_a^\top K_a\, \mathbf{p}_a.
\label{eq:per_axis_compactness}
\end{equation}
The proxy mismatch index is
\begin{equation}
\zeta(\mathbf{X}_a) \triangleq 1 - \lambda_d\, c_d - \lambda_a\, c_a,
\label{eq:struct_state}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_d, \lambda_a > 0$ with $\lambda_d + \lambda_a = 1$.
The proxy requires only marginalization and two matrix--vector products, reducing cost to $\mathcal{O}(N_a N_t + N_a^2 + N_t^2)$.
Table~\ref{tab:zeta_runtime} summarizes the computational comparison.

Since $\zeta$ serves solely as a lookup key for the policy map $\mu$ in \eqref{eq:policy_map}, only the rank ordering of realizations needs to be preserved; the mapping is invariant to monotone transformations of $\zeta$.
We adopt equal weights $\lambda_d = \lambda_a = 1/2$ as the default.
Alternative choices include dimension-weighted ($\lambda_d = N_t/(N_a+N_t)$, $\lambda_a = N_a/(N_a+N_t)$) and OLS-fitted weights minimizing $\sum_i(\zeta_{\mathrm{full},i} - \zeta_{\mathrm{proxy},i})^2$ on the training set; these are compared in ablation.

\textbf{Remark} (Data-driven weight optimization).
The constraint $\lambda_d + \lambda_a = 1$ defines a one-dimensional family.
Given profiling data, the weights can be optimized as
$(\lambda_d^*, \lambda_a^*) = \argmin_{\lambda_d + \lambda_a = 1} \sum_{i}(\zeta_{\mathrm{full},i} - \zeta_{\mathrm{proxy},i})^2$,
or more directly by maximizing Spearman rank correlation with $\zeta_{\mathrm{full}}$.
This data-driven extension is left for future investigation.

\subsection{Downstream Reliability Shortfall}
\label{subsec:shortfall}

Let $\pi_{\mathrm{anc}}$ denote a high-precision anchor policy with reference rate $r_i^{\mathrm{ref}}=r(\mathbf{X}_i;\pi_{\mathrm{anc}})$.
For reliability target $\gamma\in(0,1)$, the normalized shortfall under policy $\pi$ is
\begin{equation}
V_i(\pi;\gamma) = \frac{[\gamma\, r_i^{\mathrm{ref}} - r_i(\pi)]_+}{\gamma\, r_i^{\mathrm{ref}} + \eta_r},
\label{eq:shortfall}
\end{equation}
where $[\cdot]_+=\max(\cdot,0)$ and $\eta_r>0$ prevents division by zero.
This single primitive naturally captures both NMSE-sensitive and rate-critical regimes without manual switching: when quantization is mild, shortfall penalizes NMSE-like degradation; under aggressive quantization, it captures beam-critical rate violations.

\subsection{State-Conditioned Importance Surface}
\label{subsec:importance_surface}

Let $\pi^{(m\to b)}$ denote the policy changing only block $m$ to bit-width $b$ while keeping all others at anchor precision. The marginal shortfall increase is
\begin{equation}
\Delta V_i(m,b;\gamma) = V_i(\pi^{(m\to b)};\gamma) - V_i(\pi_{\mathrm{anc}};\gamma).
\label{eq:marginal_shortfall}
\end{equation}
The state-conditioned importance surface is defined as
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{m,b}(\boldsymbol{\xi}) \triangleq \E\!\left[
\Delta V_i(m,b;\gamma) \Given \boldsymbol{\xi}_i = \boldsymbol{\xi}
\right],
\label{eq:importance_surface}
\end{equation}
measuring the expected reliability loss increase from assigning block $m$ to bit-width $b$, conditioned on wireless state $\boldsymbol{\xi}=(\rho,\zeta)$.

This is the core quantity of RP-MPQ. Because $\Omega_{m,b}(\boldsymbol{\xi})$ is conditioned on both SNR and CSI structure, it automatically encodes regime-dependent behavior: blocks that are NMSE-sensitive under high SNR and blocks that are rate-critical under diffuse structure are distinguished by the surface without manual regime separation.

\subsection{Importance Estimation}
\label{subsec:estimation}

The state space $(\rho,\zeta)$ is discretized into a $J\times K$ grid of quantile cells $\{C_{j,k}\}$, with $J$ bins along $\rho$ and $K$ bins along $\zeta$.

\textbf{Raw estimator.}
Within each cell $(j,k)$, the raw importance is the cell-wise average:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\Omega}_{m,b}^{\mathrm{raw},(j,k)} = \frac{1}{N_{jk}} \sum_{i:\,\boldsymbol{\xi}_i\in C_{j,k}} \Delta V_i(m,b;\gamma).
\label{eq:raw_omega}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Shrinkage stabilization.}
For cells with limited samples, we shrink toward the global block-bit mean:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\Omega}_{m,b}^{(j,k)} = \alpha_{jk}\,\widehat{\Omega}_{m,b}^{\mathrm{raw},(j,k)} + (1-\alpha_{jk})\,\bar{\Omega}_{m,b},
\label{eq:shrinkage}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\Omega}_{m,b} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\Delta V_i(m,b;\gamma)$ is the global mean and $\alpha_{jk}=N_{jk}/(N_{jk}+\tau_{\mathrm{shr}})$ controls shrinkage intensity.

\textbf{Monotone calibration.}
A 2-D isotonic calibration is applied to enforce physical monotonicity:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Nonincreasing in $\rho$}: higher SNR reduces quantization sensitivity.
\item \emph{Nondecreasing in $\zeta$}: diffuse CSI structure increases quantization sensitivity.
\end{itemize}
At runtime, bilinear interpolation or direct cell lookup yields $\widehat{\Omega}_{m,b}(\boldsymbol{\xi}_t)$.

\subsection{State-Wise Policy Optimization}
\label{subsec:policy_opt}

Under an additive surrogate assumption, the optimal policy for each state cell minimizes
\begin{equation}
\pi^*(\boldsymbol{\xi}) = \argmin_{\pi\in\Pi}\left[
\sum_{m=1}^{M} \widehat{\Omega}_{m,\pi_m}(\boldsymbol{\xi}) + \lambda\,\kappa_\pi
\right],
\label{eq:policy_opt}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda\ge 0$ balances reliability loss against compute cost.
The Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$ is calibrated offline via bisection to satisfy the long-term budget constraint $\E[\kappa_{\pi_t}]\le\bar{c}$.

Since the additive surrogate decouples across blocks, the per-state optimization in \eqref{eq:policy_opt} decomposes into $M$ independent block-level minimizations for a given $\lambda$, yielding $\mathcal{O}(M|\mathcal{B}|)$ complexity per state cell. The complete offline stage produces a state-to-policy map
\begin{equation}
\mu(\boldsymbol{\xi}) = \pi^*(\boldsymbol{\xi}),
\label{eq:policy_map}
\end{equation}
stored as a lookup table over the $J\times K$ grid.

\section{RP-MPQ: Online Deployment}
\label{sec:online}
% =========================================================

Given the offline-constructed policy map $\mu$ in \eqref{eq:policy_map}, the UE performs a single table lookup per CSI realization.

\subsection{Online Decision Rule}
\label{subsec:online_rule}

At runtime, the UE computes the current wireless state $\boldsymbol{\xi}_t=(\rho_t,\zeta(\mathbf{X}_t))$, looks up the pre-computed policy $\pi_t=\mu(\boldsymbol{\xi}_t)$, and executes the encoder under the selected mixed-precision policy.
No online optimization is performed: the entire adaptation is reduced to a single table lookup.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:online_rpmpq} summarizes the procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{RP-MPQ: Online Policy Deployment}
\label{alg:online_rpmpq}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE CSI $\mathbf{X}_a$, SNR $\rho_t$, offline policy map $\mu$
\STATE Compute $\zeta_t = \zeta(\mathbf{X}_a)$ via \eqref{eq:struct_state}
\STATE Form wireless state $\boldsymbol{\xi}_t = (\rho_t,\, \zeta_t)$
\STATE Look up policy $\pi_t \leftarrow \mu(\boldsymbol{\xi}_t)$
\STATE Execute encoder $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_t = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}_a;\, \pi_t)$
\RETURN quantized feedback $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_t$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Online Complexity}
\label{subsec:online_complexity}

The online cost consists of computing $\zeta(\mathbf{X}_t)$ via \eqref{eq:struct_state} ($\mathcal{O}(N_a N_t + N_a^2 + N_t^2)$ for the proxy; see Table~\ref{tab:zeta_runtime}) and a table lookup ($\mathcal{O}(1)$), both negligible relative to encoder inference. Therefore, RP-MPQ enables reliability-aware mixed-precision adaptation with no measurable overhead.

\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:experiments}
% =========================================================
This section validates the proposed asymmetric CSI feedback framework with RP-MPQ along three dimensions: (i) structural efficiency of the asymmetric UE encoder, (ii) effectiveness of the offline mixed-precision policy construction, and (iii) reliability-aware online adaptation under identical long-term average UE-side compute budgets.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:exp_setup}
We consider an FDD massive MIMO downlink system with $N_t=32$ transmit antennas at the BS and a single-antenna UE ($N_r=1$). CSI is represented in the delay--angular domain with $N_f=1024$ OFDM subcarriers, and only the first $N_a=32$ delay taps are retained for feedback. The BS employs maximum ratio transmission (MRT) based on reconstructed CSI, and performance is evaluated at SNR levels of 10, 20, and 30~dB.

We use COST~2100 outdoor channel realizations \cite{COST2100} (carrier frequency $f_c=300$~MHz) and additionally evaluate generalization on FR1 TDL-A/B/C profiles from 3GPP TR~38.901 \cite{3GPP38901}. The UE encoder is a Mamba-style selective SSM with 2-D cross-scan \cite{Mamba, VMamba}, and the BS decoder is Transformer-based. Training uses AdamW with learning rate $10^{-3}$ for 200 epochs. Mixed-precision quantization is applied to UE-side encoder weights with candidate bit-widths $\{16,8,4,2\}$, while internal activations use 16-bit and the latent uses 8-bit quantization for feedback. Unless stated otherwise, complexity is reported as encoder FLOPs (full precision) or encoder BOPs (quantized inference), normalized consistently across methods. \subsection{Online RP-MPQ: Reliability under Identical Budgets}
\label{subsec:online_outage}
We compare uniform precision, static mixed precision (offline-only), and online RP-MPQ under identical \emph{average} UE-side encoder BOP budgets. Reliability is evaluated via outage probability under targets $\gamma \in \{0.99, 0.98, 0.95\}$ at SNR levels of 10, 20, and 30~dB.

\subsubsection{Transmission Rate and Outage Definition}
To compute per-subcarrier MRT precoding, the reconstructed delay--angular CSI $\hat{\mathbf{X}}_a$ is mapped back to the frequency domain by inverse angular transforms and a DFT across delay taps. Specifically, we form $\hat{\mathbf{H}}_d[\ell]=\mathbf{F}_r^{\mathsf{H}}\hat{\mathbf{X}}[\ell]\mathbf{F}_t$ for $\ell=0,\ldots,N_a-1$, zero-pad $\hat{\mathbf{H}}_d[\ell]=\mathbf{0}$ for $\ell\ge N_a$, and compute $\hat{\mathbf{H}}[n]=\frac{1}{\sqrt{N_f}}\sum_{\ell=0}^{N_f-1}\hat{\mathbf{H}}_d[\ell]e^{-j2\pi n\ell/N_f}$. For $N_r=1$, the corresponding channel vector is $\hat{\mathbf{h}}[n]\in\mathbb{C}^{N_t}$. For the $t$-th CSI realization, the achievable rate is defined as
\begin{equation}
r_t(\mathbf{x})
\triangleq
\frac{1}{N_f}\sum_{n=0}^{N_f-1}
\log_2\!\left(1+\rho \abs{\mathbf{h}_t[n]^{\mathsf{H}} \mathbf{w}_{\mathbf{x}}[n]}^2 \right),
\label{eq:rate_def}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{h}_t[n]$ is the true channel, $\rho$ is the received SNR, and $\mathbf{w}_{\mathbf{x}}[n] \triangleq \mathbf{x}[n]/\norm{\mathbf{x}[n]}_2$ is the MRT beamforming vector. Equal power allocation across subcarriers is assumed.

Under policy $\pi$, let $\hat{\mathbf{h}}_{t,\pi}[n]$ denote the reconstructed channel used to form the MRT precoder, and define $r_t(\pi) \triangleq r_t(\hat{\mathbf{h}}_{t,\pi})$. The reference rate is $r_t^{\mathrm{ref}} \triangleq r_t(\mathbf{h}_t^{(a)})$, where $\mathbf{h}_t^{(a)}[n]$ is obtained from the truncated ground-truth $\mathbf{X}_a$ via the same inverse transforms (with zero-padding), so that $r_t^{\mathrm{ref}}$ does not penalize the representation truncation itself. An outage event is declared when
\begin{equation}
r_t(\pi) < \gamma\, r_t^{\mathrm{ref}}.
\label{eq:outage_event}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Outage Performance under Online RP-MPQ}
Fig.~\ref{fig:outage_final} presents rate-based outage probability versus BOPs saving budget for three SNR levels and three reliability thresholds ($\gamma \in \{0.99, 0.98, 0.95\}$). Online RP-MPQ (solid) consistently shifts the outage cliff to higher savings compared to static MP (dashed), demonstrating that per-sample policy adaptation reduces outage events without increasing the average UE-side encoder compute.
This empirical gain aligns with the SSM encoder's inherent quantization robustness: the contractive state recursion limits perturbation amplification, enabling reliable operation at aggressive saving levels.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =========================================================
This paper studied CSI feedback for FDD massive MIMO under UE-side inference constraints.
An asymmetric autoencoder pairing a lightweight SSM encoder at the UE with a Transformer decoder at the BS provides a quantization-robust baseline, while RP-MPQ constructs per-state optimal policies offline via a state-conditioned reliability importance surface and deploys them online via pure table lookup under a long-term UE compute budget. Experimental results demonstrated an improved accuracy--complexity trade-off and reduced rate-based outage events under identical average UE-side budgets.

The current framework applies post-training quantization with fixed activation precision (INT16) and uses equal proxy weights ($\lambda_d=\lambda_a=1/2$). Extensions to multi-antenna UEs, joint weight--activation optimization, data-driven optimization of proxy weights $(\lambda_d,\lambda_a)$ under $\lambda_d+\lambda_a=1$, and non-stationary channel statistics are left for future work.
